Geriatric Medication Guide

Introduction: Navigating Medications and Supplements in Older Adulthood

Geriatric and Drugs by Sungjoon Jun, PharmD

The healthcare journey for older adults is often complex, frequently involving multiple chronic conditions that require numerous medications. This use of multiple medications, known as polypharmacy, creates significant hurdles in managing health safely and effectively, impacting overall well-being. As we age, our bodies change, affecting how we absorb, process, and eliminate drugs. These changes can lead to unexpected reactions and a higher chance of negative side effects. Additionally, the sheer number of pills, or "pill burden," can feel overwhelming, causing confusion, missed doses, and potential harm. "Pill burden" encompasses not just the quantity of medications but also the total effort involved in managing them, including storing, organizing, taking, and understanding each one.

This guide aims to help older adults and their caregivers understand the critical aspects of using medications and supplements. It covers the risks of certain common drugs, suggests safer options, provides tips for managing costs and taking medicines correctly, explains brand versus generic drugs, discusses prescription addiction concerns, simplifies drug categories, offers solutions for swallowing difficulties, and provides a guide to over-the-counter (OTC) choices. Our goal is to empower older adults to make well-informed decisions, promoting safer and more effective medication use.

Chapter 1: Benadryl Use in Geriatrics for Insomnia: Dangers and Alternatives

Key Takeaways:

  • Avoid Benadryl (diphenhydramine) for sleep in older adults due to high risks (confusion, falls, dementia link).
  • Tolerance to Benadryl develops quickly, but the risks remain.
  • Non-drug strategies (Sleep Hygiene, CBT-I) are often the safest and most effective long-term solutions for insomnia.
  • Discuss any OTC sleep aids (including Melatonin or Magnesium) with your doctor first.

Did you know?

Your body can become tolerant to the sleep-inducing effects of Benadryl in as little as one to two weeks, but the risky side effects don't go away! This means it stops helping you sleep but can still cause confusion or dizziness.

1.1 The Risks of Diphenhydramine (Benadryl) in Older Adults

Diphenhydramine, widely recognized by its brand name Benadryl, is an antihistamine available without a prescription. While often used for occasional sleeplessness due to its drowsy-making properties, it is strongly advised against for treating long-term insomnia, especially in older adults.

A major issue is that the body quickly builds tolerance to its sleep-inducing effects, often within just one or two weeks of continuous use. This rapid development of tolerance means its sleep benefit fades fast, while the risks associated with the drug persist, creating a hazardous situation.

The primary reason to avoid Benadryl in older adults is its strong "anticholinergic" effects. Anticholinergic drugs block acetylcholine, a vital chemical messenger in the brain essential for memory, learning, and focus. In older individuals, whose bodies might not clear these drugs as efficiently, these effects can lead to serious side effects. Research strongly links the long-term use of anticholinergic drugs like diphenhydramine to an increased risk of dementia, a risk not limited to older adults but also potentially affecting younger individuals. These drugs can impair cognitive functions such as alertness, attention, memory, and reaction time. One study indicated that older hospital patients using diphenhydramine had a significantly higher risk of delirium (sudden confusion), including difficulties with attention and disorganized speech.

Beyond cognitive effects, diphenhydramine can cause various physical problems in older adults:

  • Daytime sleepiness
  • Dizziness
  • Dry mouth
  • Constipation
  • Difficulty urinating completely
  • Blurred vision
  • Increased risk of falls due to sleepiness and dizziness
  • Balance and coordination issues
  • Mucus buildup in the lungs
  • Fast heart rate (tachycardia)
  • Low blood pressure (hypotension)

Older adults with kidney or liver problems are particularly vulnerable because their bodies cannot process the drug effectively. Due to these significant risks, the American Geriatrics Society (AGS) Beers Criteria® lists diphenhydramine as a medication that older adults should generally avoid.

1.2 Alternative OTC and Natural Options for Insomnia

Given the risks associated with Benadryl, exploring safer sleep aids is crucial. Often, the most effective approach involves strategies that do not rely on drugs.

Over-the-Counter (OTC) & Natural Alternatives:

  • Melatonin: This natural hormone helps regulate the sleep-wake cycle. Supplements may aid with jet lag or falling asleep, though the effect is typically mild. Studies suggest it can improve sleep quality and appears safe for short-term use. While not considered physically addictive like prescription sleep aids, some individuals may develop a psychological reliance or experience tolerance, necessitating higher doses over time. It is best used short-term and under medical guidance. Potential side effects include headaches, nausea, or daytime sleepiness. Doses usually range from 3 to 10 milligrams (mg) before bedtime.
  • Magnesium: Some research indicates that magnesium supplements may help improve sleep quality, particularly in older adults, possibly by aiding in neurotransmitter regulation and muscle relaxation. Study dosages have varied, with one demonstrating benefits at 500mg daily. However, consulting a doctor before starting magnesium is essential, as it can cause side effects like diarrhea and cramping and may interact with other medications.
  • Valerian Root: This herbal supplement is sometimes used for sleep. Some studies suggest it offers benefits, especially for women in menopause, but research findings are inconsistent. Side effects are generally mild (e.g., headache, weakness), and while short-term use seems safe, long-term safety is not well-established.

Note on Doxylamine (Unisom): Similar to Benadryl, doxylamine (Unisom) is a sedating antihistamine. It carries comparable risks for older adults, including drowsiness, dry mouth, and constipation, and is also listed on the Beers Criteria® as potentially inappropriate. Therefore, it should be used with extreme caution, if at all, in this population.

Non-Pharmacological and Natural Strategies:

These methods, focusing on habits and environment, often provide lasting benefits without the side effects associated with medication.

  • Sleep Hygiene: This involves cultivating good sleep habits.
    • Maintain a regular sleep-wake schedule, even on weekends.
    • Ensure your bedroom is cool (60-70°F), dark, quiet, and comfortable.
    • Avoid large meals, caffeine, nicotine, and alcohol close to bedtime.
    • Limit naps to under an hour and take them earlier in the day.
    • Engage in relaxing activities like reading or listening to music to wind down before bed.
    • Avoid screens (phones, TVs, computers) for at least an hour before sleep.
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I): This is a highly recommended therapy for chronic insomnia. It helps modify unhelpful beliefs and attitudes about sleep and includes:
    • Stimulus Control: Training to associate the bed only with sleep and intimacy. If unable to sleep within 15-20 minutes, get out of bed, go to another room, and only return when sleepy.
    • Sleep Restriction: Limiting time in bed to match the actual duration of sleep, then gradually increasing it as sleep improves. This should be used cautiously if you have conditions like epilepsy.
    • Relaxation Training: Employing techniques such as muscle relaxation or meditation to calm the mind and body.
  • Dietary Adjustments: Some individuals find chamomile tea, warm milk, or tart cherry juice helpful for promoting sleep.

Utilizing these non-drug strategies can significantly improve sleep for older adults, often more effectively and safely than medication.

Chapter 2: Medications to Avoid and Safer Alternatives

Key Takeaways:

  • The Beers Criteria® helps identify risky medications for older adults.
  • Many common drug classes, such as first-generation antihistamines, certain antidepressants, benzodiazepines, and NSAIDs, pose higher risks for older individuals.
  • Always discuss potential alternatives with your doctor or pharmacist.
  • Never assume a medication is safe merely because it's widely used or available over-the-counter.

2.1 Principles for Avoiding Medications in Older Adults

The American Geriatrics Society (AGS) Beers Criteria® is a crucial resource for minimizing drug-related problems in adults aged 65 and older. It identifies Potentially Inappropriate Medications (PIMs)—drugs that should be avoided, used cautiously, monitored for interactions, avoided in specific conditions, or adjusted for kidney function. This tool assists doctors and patients in selecting safer medications and enhancing the quality of care.

The inclusion of a drug on the Beers list does not mean that no older adult should ever take it. Rather, it signifies that these drugs carry higher risks or may not be as effective due to age-related physiological changes and the multiple medications older adults often consume. The way the body handles drugs changes with age, making responses difficult to predict. Treatment must always be personalized, taking into account individual health goals and preferences.

Doctors should exercise caution when prescribing these medications, ensuring a clear need exists and that the benefits outweigh the potential risks. Regular medication reviews are vital to eliminate unneeded drugs (a process known as deprescribing) and to check for interactions or necessary dosage adjustments. Patients and caregivers must share a complete list of all medications, including OTC drugs and supplements, and report any concerns to their healthcare providers.

Chapter 3: Finding Cheaper Drug Prices

Key Takeaways:

  • Don't skip medications due to cost; many assistance programs exist.
  • Always ask for generic options and compare prices.
  • Explore cash-pay options or pharmacy programs – they can sometimes be cheaper than insurance.
  • Talk openly with your healthcare team about cost concerns.

Medication costs can be a significant issue, sometimes leading individuals to skip doses. Fortunately, options exist to help lower these costs.

Government and Non-Profit Programs:

  • Extra Help (Low-Income Subsidy): A federal program assisting Medicare users with limited income in paying for Part D drug costs. Enrollment may be automatic, or an application via Social Security may be required.
  • Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs): State programs that help pay Medicare Part A/B costs, thereby freeing up funds for medications.
  • State Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (SPAPs): Many states offer these programs to help cover Medicare Part D costs not paid by Medicare.
  • Charitable Foundations: Organizations like the PAN Foundation assist eligible patients with out-of-pocket medication costs.
  • FundFinder: A free web application from the PAN Foundation that helps locate assistance from over 200 programs.

Private Sector and General Strategies:

  • Manufacturer Programs (PAPs): Drug companies often have programs offering free or low-cost medications, though rules can be strict for Medicare users. Check company websites or NeedyMeds.org for information.
  • Choose Generic Drugs: Generics contain the same active ingredient and work identically to brand-name drugs but often cost 80-85% less. Always inquire if a generic alternative is available.
  • Prescription Discount Cards/Apps: Services provide coupons and price comparisons across pharmacies, often resulting in significant savings, especially for those without insurance or with high deductibles. Their websites or apps can be used to find the best local prices.
  • Pharmacy Price Matching: Some pharmacies may match or accept prices from discount cards, even if not directly contracted. It is always worthwhile to ask your pharmacist if they can match a lower price you have found.
  • Costco Pharmacy: A Costco membership is not required to use their pharmacy and benefit from their prescription prices. Simply inform the door attendant that you are going to the pharmacy.
  • Pill Splitting: If approved by your doctor, splitting a higher-dose pill can reduce costs. This should only be done with medical guidance.
  • Combination Pills: Inquire if multiple medications can be combined into a single pill, potentially lowering co-pays.
  • Buy in Bulk/Mail-Order: Mail-order pharmacies might offer larger supplies for less money. Many pharmacies and health plans offer home delivery for prescriptions. Some services ship medications already sorted into daily packets, which can replace traditional pill organizers and eliminate trips to pick up refills.
  • Talk to Your Doctor/Pharmacist: If cost is an issue, inform them. They may be aware of cheaper alternatives or assistance programs.

Chapter 4: Resources for Adherence and Management

Key Takeaways:

  • Taking medications correctly is key to managing health conditions.
  • Use tools like pill organizers, apps, and calendars to help remember doses.
  • Simplify your regimen with your doctor whenever possible.
  • Educate yourself about your medications and don't hesitate to ask for help.

Taking medications as prescribed (adherence) is vital, especially for older adults with chronic conditions. Complex schedules, memory issues, and cost can make adherence challenging. Remembering to take medicine on time is crucial for it to work properly. Fortunately, many medication reminder tools – both high-tech and low-tech – can help remember doses and “check off” that they were taken.

4.1 Strategies for Medication Adherence

  • Simplify Regimens: Doctors can help by discontinuing unneeded drugs (deprescribing) and choosing once-daily options when feasible.
  • Regular Medication Reviews: Have all your medicines (prescriptions, OTC, supplements) reviewed by a healthcare professional to identify potential problems.
  • Involve Caregivers: Family members or caregivers can assist with organizing pills, setting reminders, and communicating with doctors. A caregiver can help by pre-organizing pills, reading labels if vision is poor, and tracking refills. Caregivers often use tools like calendars, pill organizers, and phone alarms on the patient's behalf. For example, a family member might set up a pillbox weekly and also set a daily phone alarm. A spouse might bring the pillbox each morning and observe the medication being taken.
  • Routine and Habit Cues: Link taking medication with daily routines. For instance, if you always have coffee at 7 AM, keep your morning pillbox near the coffee maker (in a safe place away from children). The routine activity will serve as a reminder. Some people flip the pill bottle upside down after taking the dose and turn it right-side up later, providing a visual indicator.
  • Family or Buddy System: A quick daily phone call from a family member or friend can be a helpful reminder. Some seniors arrange a system with a neighbor or friend for medication check-in calls. This can be combined with a personal checklist.
  • Keep an Updated Medication List: Maintain an updated medication list in a visible place, like on the fridge, including each drug’s name, dose, and time. This helps follow the schedule and is useful for caregivers or in emergencies. Use this list to double-check weekly that the pill organizer is filled correctly.
  • Accessibility Tip: Keep medications in an easy-to-reach location, especially if you have limited mobility. A cleared section of the kitchen counter or a table near an armchair (away from direct sunlight and moisture) might be suitable. Ensure it's a secure place if children or pets are in the home. Good lighting where medications are taken is also important for reading labels clearly.

4.2 Tools and Technologies for Reminders and Management

Many tools can help manage medications effectively:

Digital Reminder Solutions (for those comfortable with simple tech):

  • Medication Management Apps (Smartphone Apps):
    • Function: Apps send reminders for taking pills and getting refills.
    • Features: They allow tracking of doses, schedules, symptoms, and appointments. Users can mark doses as "taken" in the app. Some apps can check for drug interactions or create a log to share with family, or warn if medication is running low.
    • Consideration: Ensure the app is easy for older adults to see and navigate; adjust phone text size if needed for readability.
  • Voice-Activated Assistants (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant):
    • How they work: These devices can provide audible medication reminders based on a user-set schedule via voice commands. This is useful for those with visual impairments or who prefer spoken prompts. Family members can assist with initial setup.
    • Advanced Features: Some systems can notify caregivers if a dose is missed. Apple's Health app (iOS 16+) includes a built-in feature with Siri integration.
  • Electronic Pill Dispensers and Smart Caps: These electronic pillboxes and bottle caps help track doses.
    • Automatic Pill Dispensers: Devices can beep, flash a light, or deliver a voice message at medication time. They dispense the correct pills at scheduled times, preventing the need to open multiple bottles. Some are locked dispensers that open only the correct compartment and can alert a caregiver if a dose is missed. Others use voice and visual alerts.
    • Timer Caps: A timer cap for a pill bottle displays the time the last dose was taken, helping to confirm if a pill has already been consumed.
    • Note: High-tech dispensers can be expensive, so consider the benefits versus cost and simpler options if needed.

Non-Digital (Low-Tech) Reminder Solutions (senior-friendly methods not requiring smartphones):

  • Physical Tools:
    • Pill Organizer Boxes: Weekly pill boxes are simple tools for sorting pills. Each day's compartment acts as a check. Some organizers have AM/PM sections or even alarms; choose one with large labels that is easy to open for arthritic hands.
    • Smart Pill Dispensers: Advanced devices that release the correct dose at the right time and can alert caregivers.
    • Personalized Packaging: Some pharmacies offer "blister packs" or pre-sorted pill packets.
    • Easy-Open Packaging: Request easy-open caps from the pharmacist. Rubber grip aids or cap opener devices can also help.
  • Visual Reminders (Calendars/Charts):
    • Visual Schedules: A simple chart with medication names, doses, and times posted in a visible place. Use a calendar or medication chart to check off each dose when taken.
    • Checklists: Marking off doses provides a clear record.
  • Sticky Notes and Signs: Place written reminders in prominent spots.
  • Digital Calendars: Smartphone or computer calendars can send alerts for each dose.

Other Support:

  • Home Health Services: Aides can provide reminders and help with organization.
  • Delivery & Refills: Utilize pharmacy delivery and automatic refills.
  • Free Informational Websites: Resources like the National Institute on Aging (NIA), MedlinePlus (National Library of Medicine), and NCCIH (National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health) offer valuable information.

Chapter 5: Brand vs. Generic Medications: Understanding the Differences

Key Takeaways:

  • Generic drugs are as safe and effective as brand-name drugs, according to the FDA.
  • Generics look different due to trademark laws, not because of differences in the medicine itself.
  • Choosing generics can lead to significant cost savings.
  • If your pill's appearance changes, always ask your pharmacist to confirm it's the correct medication.

Did you know?

Generic drugs are required by the FDA to have the same active ingredient, strength, and dosage form as their brand-name counterparts. They often cost 80-85% less simply because their manufacturers didn't have to pay for the initial discovery and large clinical trials!

Choosing between brand and generic drugs often raises questions about quality, effectiveness, and cost. For example, Lipitor is a brand name for a cholesterol drug; its generic name is atorvastatin.

5.1 Effectiveness and FDA Standards for Generic Drugs

All generic drugs in the U.S. must be approved by the FDA (Food & Drug Administration) as equivalent to their brand-name counterparts. The FDA mandates that "FDA-approved generic medicines work in the same way and provide the same clinical benefit and risks as their brand-name counterparts." This means a generic drug must have:

  • The exact same active pharmaceutical ingredient (API).
  • The same dosage form (e.g., tablet, capsule), strength (e.g., mg), and route of administration (e.g., oral).
  • Bioequivalence: It must deliver the same amount of medicine to the bloodstream at approximately the same rate and extent as the brand-name drug.
  • The same intended uses (conditions it treats).
  • Meet the same high standards for quality, purity, potency, and manufacturing.

Generics are typically cheaper because their manufacturers do not have to repeat the original, expensive research and large clinical trials; they only need to demonstrate bioequivalence. On average, generic drugs cost about 80%-85% less than brand-name drugs. Insurance companies, Medicare, and Medicaid almost always prefer generics due to the cost savings for both the system and the patient. From 2007–2016, the use of generics saved the U.S. healthcare system hundreds of billions of dollars. For patients, this usually translates to a much lower co-pay.

The FDA allows a very small margin of variability in absorption, but in practice, the difference between a brand and its generic, or between two generic versions, is usually minimal—often only a few percentage points and typically not clinically meaningful. Both brand and generic medications are manufactured in facilities that must adhere to strict FDA standards and inspections. Many generics are even produced by the same companies that make the brand-name drug under contract. Legally and scientifically, generics must perform the same as brands to be sold. The FDA actively monitors reports of any problems with generics, just as with brands, to ensure ongoing safety.

5.2 Why Patients May Feel a Brand Works Better or Why Pill Appearance Differs

Despite scientific confirmation of equivalence, some individuals prefer brand-name drugs or feel they work better. This can occur for several reasons:

  • Marketing and Familiarity: Companies invest heavily in promoting brand names, creating familiarity and loyalty among consumers.
  • Psychological Factors (Placebo/Nocebo Effect): If a generic pill looks different (e.g., color, shape) from the brand-name drug an individual is accustomed to, they might subconsciously feel it won't work as well, even if the active ingredient is identical. Studies have shown that changing a pill's appearance can sometimes lead people to stop taking it.
  • Inactive Ingredients: While the active medicinal component is the same, the inactive ingredients (fillers, dyes, binders, coatings) can differ between brand and generic versions, and even between different manufacturers of the same generic drug. Although these are regulated, rare sensitivities or allergies to an inactive ingredient might cause side effects in some individuals, leading them to prefer the brand or a specific generic formulation.
  • Switching Between Generic Manufacturers: If a patient switches between different generic manufacturers, they might perceive variations.

The reason pill color and shape differ is primarily due to trademark law. Brand-name companies often trademark the appearance (shape, color) of their pills. Unlike patents on the drug itself (which expire, allowing generics to be made), trademarks on appearance can last indefinitely. Therefore, when generic companies manufacture the same medicine, they cannot copy the exact look of the brand-name pill. Each generic manufacturer typically chooses its own distinct appearance for its version of the drug. Pharmacies might also change their generic drug suppliers, leading to patients receiving pills that look different from one refill to the next.

While this can be confusing, especially if one relies on visual cues to identify pills, it can be managed by:

  • Using pill organizers.
  • Using medication reminders.
  • Always asking your pharmacist to confirm you have the correct medicine if its appearance changes.
  • Keeping an updated medication list.

5.3 When a Brand-Name Medication Might Be Necessary

Occasionally, a patient may have a specific medical reason to use a brand-name drug (e.g., a documented allergy to an inactive ingredient in all available generics, or if a doctor determines that the small variance in a generic could significantly affect treatment for a very narrow therapeutic index drug, where tiny changes in blood level matter, like some thyroid or seizure medications).

In such cases:

  1. Discuss with your doctor: Explain your concerns or experiences with the generic.
  2. "Dispense as Written" (DAW): If the doctor agrees the brand is medically necessary, they must write the prescription accordingly, often by indicating "Dispense as Written (DAW)" or "Brand Medically Necessary." This instructs the pharmacist not to substitute a generic. Many U.S. states have laws allowing or requiring pharmacists to substitute a generic unless the prescription explicitly prohibits it.
  3. Prior Authorization: Your insurance company will likely require prior authorization for the brand-name drug if a generic is available. This means the doctor's office must submit a request to the insurer explaining why the generic cannot be used. The insurer reviews this (which can take days to weeks) and decides on coverage. Approval may be granted for documented adverse reactions to generics or other valid medical reasons. If denied, an appeal is possible, but success is not guaranteed.
  4. Cost Implications: Without insurance coverage, the brand-name medicine will be significantly more expensive out-of-pocket. Insurance plans often have formulary tiers, with generics in Tier 1 (lowest cost) and brands in higher, more expensive tiers. Some plans may not cover a brand at all if a generic alternative exists, barring exceptional medical necessity. If paying out-of-pocket for a brand-name drug, check if the manufacturer offers patient assistance programs or discount coupons, though Medicare and Medicaid patients are usually ineligible for manufacturer coupons due to federal rules.

Bottom line: For most people, generic medicines are equally effective as brand-names and offer substantial cost savings. If you believe a brand drug is better for you, discuss it thoroughly with your doctor, weighing any perceived benefits against the likely higher cost and insurance hurdles. In many instances, a trial period may show that the generic performs just as well. For example, Tylenol (brand) and acetaminophen (generic) are the same drug; the generic simply costs less.

Chapter 6: Concerns of Addiction to Prescription Drugs

Key Takeaways:

  • Be aware of addiction risks, particularly with opioids and benzodiazepines.
  • Understanding DEA schedules helps in managing risks and handling medications safely.
  • Never share controlled substances.
  • Utilize drug take-back programs for disposal; flush Fentanyl patches (folded) and other "Flush List" drugs only if no take-back option is available.

Did you know?

Prescription drug misuse in older adults is often unintentional and can be harder to spot as some warning signs (like confusion or falls) can mimic normal aging. Open communication with doctors is key.

6.1 Identifying Addiction Concerns in Older Adults

Prescription drug misuse and addiction in older adults is a hidden yet serious issue. It often occurs accidentally due to the use of multiple medications and age-related physiological changes. Over half of individuals aged 55-85 take five or more prescription drugs daily.

Factors increasing risk include:

  • Polypharmacy: More medications increase the risk of interactions and dependence.
  • Age-Related Changes: Slower drug processing means dependence can occur at lower doses.
  • Coping Mechanisms: Loneliness, grief, or chronic pain can lead to using medications to cope.
  • Alcohol: Alcohol can interact dangerously with many medications.

Commonly misused drugs include Opioids (e.g., Oxycodone, Hydrocodone) for pain and Benzodiazepines (e.g., Valium, Xanax, Ativan) for anxiety or sleep.

Warning Signs (which can sometimes mimic signs of aging):

  • Behavioral: New irritability, secretiveness, defensiveness.
  • Cognitive: New confusion, memory loss, attention issues.
  • Physical: More falls, balance problems, changes in sleep patterns or appetite.
  • Prescription-Seeking Behavior: "Doctor shopping," filling prescriptions at multiple pharmacies, taking more medication than prescribed.

If you suspect misuse, contact the doctor immediately. Honest communication about all substances used, including alcohol, is crucial for safe treatment.

6.2 DEA Drug Schedules

The Controlled Substances Act (CSA) establishes rules for drugs that have a potential for abuse. The Drug Enforcement Administration (DEA) categorizes these drugs into five "Schedules" (I through V) based on their accepted medical use and potential for abuse or dependence. Lower schedule numbers indicate a higher risk and stricter regulations.

  • Schedule I: High abuse potential, no currently accepted medical use in the U.S.
    • Examples: Heroin, LSD, Marijuana (cannabis), Ecstasy.
    • Implication: Illegal for general use.
  • Schedule II: High abuse potential (can lead to severe psychological or physical dependence), but do have accepted medical uses.
    • Examples: Fentanyl, Oxycodone (OxyContin), Morphine, Ritalin, Adderall.
    • Implication: Very strict prescribing rules; no refills typically allowed without a new prescription.
  • Schedule III: Moderate to low potential for physical and psychological dependence, accepted medical uses.
    • Examples: Anabolic steroids, Testosterone, Ketamine, products containing less than 90 milligrams of codeine per dosage unit (e.g., Tylenol with Codeine).
    • Implication: Less strict than Schedule II; refills allowed within a 6-month period.
  • Schedule IV: Low potential for abuse and low risk of dependence relative to Schedule III drugs, accepted medical uses.
    • Examples: Diazepam (Valium), Alprazolam (Xanax), Tramadol (Ultram).
    • Implication: Many anxiety and sleep medications fall into this category; still potential for dependence.
  • Schedule V: Lowest abuse potential relative to Schedule IV drugs, accepted medical uses. Contain limited quantities of certain narcotics.
    • Examples: Pregabalin (Lyrica), Lomotil, some cough syrups with codeine.
    • Implication: Least restrictive; some may be available over-the-counter in certain states, though still regulated.

Only DEA-registered practitioners can prescribe these controlled substances, ensuring oversight.

6.3 Why Understanding DEA Schedules Matters

When your doctor prescribes a controlled substance (usually Schedule II-V), understanding its schedule helps in managing it safely. This knowledge is not meant to cause fear but to inform, enabling responsible medication handling and informed discussions about risks and benefits.

  • Risk Awareness: The schedule indicates the drug's potential for misuse or dependence. This is important information for making informed decisions with your doctor.
  • Safe Handling & Storage: Knowing a medication is controlled emphasizes the need to store it securely, especially if children or teenagers are in the household, to prevent accidental exposure or misuse. Keep these drugs out of sight and preferably locked up.
  • Understanding Regulations: It explains why obtaining refills for Schedule II drugs is difficult and why doctors and pharmacists monitor these prescriptions closely.
  • Open Communication: It encourages open conversations with your doctor about pain management, anxiety, or sleep, and any concerns about taking these medications.

The goal is to use these necessary medications effectively while minimizing risks to yourself and others.

6.4 Safe Disposal of Controlled Substances

Properly disposing of unused or expired controlled substances, especially strong pain medications like patches, is crucial for safety.

Best Option: Drug Take-Back Programs:

  • The safest method is to take medications to an authorized collection site.
  • Many pharmacies, hospitals, or police stations have permanent drop-off boxes.
  • The DEA holds periodic National Prescription Drug Take-Back Days.
  • Visit the DEA's website or call to find a nearby location.
  • Mail-Back Envelopes: Some pharmacies offer or sell prepaid mail-back envelopes for drug disposal.

Disposal for Patches (e.g., Fentanyl):

  • Used patches still contain potent medicine.
  • As soon as a patch is removed, fold it in half so the sticky sides adhere to each other.
  • Crucially, check the medication's instructions. Many potent patches, like Fentanyl, are on the FDA's "Flush List" and should be flushed down the toilet immediately after folding to prevent accidental exposure if no take-back program is readily available.

In-Home Disposal (If No Other Option is Readily Available):

  1. First, check if your medication is on the FDA's "Flush List." Only flush these specific, high-risk drugs if a take-back option isn't immediately available.
  2. If the medication is not on the flush list and no take-back option is possible:
    • Remove the medicine from its original container (scratch out personal information first).
    • Mix it (do not crush tablets or capsules unless specifically instructed to do so by a pharmacist for disposal) with an unappealing substance like used coffee grounds, dirt, or kitty litter. This makes it less attractive to children, pets, or individuals seeking drugs.
    • Place the mixture in a sealed container, such as a plastic bag.
    • Throw it in your household trash.

Never share controlled substances.

Key Takeaways:

  • Taking many medications (polypharmacy) increases health risks.
  • Regular medication reviews with your doctor or pharmacist are essential.
  • Keep an accurate, updated medication list and share it at every medical visit.

Did you know?

Taking five or more medications daily (polypharmacy) is common in older adults and significantly increases the risk of harmful drug interactions, side effects, and even falls. An annual medication review can help identify and stop unnecessary drugs.

7.1 Understanding Polypharmacy and Pill Burden

Polypharmacy refers to the concurrent use of multiple medicines, often defined as five or more. It is common in older adults who manage several health conditions. "Pill burden" signifies more than just the number of pills; it encompasses the total effort of managing them—storing, organizing, taking, and understanding each medication. Taking numerous pills daily can be overwhelming, potentially leading to missed doses, medication errors, and other problems.

7.2 Risks and Importance of Regular Medication Review

Polypharmacy carries significant risks:

  • Adverse Drug Events (ADEs): Older adults have a higher susceptibility to negative reactions due to age-related physiological changes and the cumulative effect of multiple drugs.
  • Drug Interactions: Medications can interact with each other or exacerbate other health conditions.
  • Cognitive/Functional Decline: Polypharmacy can contribute to confusion, memory problems, and an increased risk of falls. Some medications are linked to adverse brain changes.
  • Falls/Fractures: Dizziness and balance issues stemming from medications elevate the risk of falls.
  • Lower Adherence: The complexity of managing multiple medications makes it harder to take them correctly.
  • Higher Healthcare Costs: Complications from polypharmacy can lead to more hospitalizations and doctor visits.

Due to these risks, regular medication reviews are essential. A healthcare professional (doctor or pharmacist) should assess all medications—including prescriptions, OTC drugs, and supplements—at least once a year, or after major health events like hospitalization. This review helps to:

  • Reduce inappropriate medications (deprescribing).
  • Improve adherence.
  • Prevent adverse reactions.
  • Optimize treatment and safely discontinue unneeded drugs.
  • Enhance communication between patient and provider.

Always bring all your medication bottles or a complete, updated list to your appointments.

Chapter 8: Options for Those Who Can't Swallow Hard Pills and Capsules

Key Takeaways:

  • Your pharmacist is a key resource—ask them questions!
  • Never crush or chew pills unless your pharmacist explicitly states it is safe to do so.

Difficulty swallowing pills (dysphagia) is common in older adults and can pose risks if not managed properly.

8.1 Compounding Pharmacy Services

If swallowing pills is challenging, a compounding pharmacy can be a valuable resource. These specialized pharmacies create personalized medications based on individual needs when a suitable FDA-approved version is not available or appropriate. They can help by:

  • Making Liquid Versions: Converting a solid pill into a liquid suspension or solution, making it much easier to swallow.
  • Changing the Form: Potentially creating alternative dosage forms, such as topical creams or gels (for certain medications), or even flavored lozenges or troches.
  • Adjusting Doses: Creating specific strengths if a commercially available dosage is not suitable.
  • Removing Allergens: Formulating medications without specific fillers, dyes, or allergens if a patient has a known sensitivity to an ingredient in a standard pill.
  • Adding Flavors: Making unpalatable medications, especially liquids, more pleasant to take.

It is important to use compounding services only when an FDA-approved medication is not suitable and always under a doctor's prescription. Discuss with your doctor or pharmacist to determine if a compounding pharmacy could be beneficial.

Always ask your pharmacist before crushing or chewing any pill.

  • Transdermal Patches: These deliver medication through the skin over a period of time.
  • Suppositories: These are inserted rectally or vaginally.
  • Injectable Medications: Some medications can be given by injection, sometimes with less frequent dosing schedules.

Chapter 9: OTC Buying Guide for Geriatrics

Key Takeaways:

  • Always consult a healthcare professional before taking any new OTC product, vitamin, or herb.
  • Read "Drug Facts" labels carefully to avoid duplicate ingredients and identify potentially inappropriate substances for older adults.
  • Be especially cautious with first-generation antihistamines and the long-term use of NSAIDs and PPIs.
  • "Natural" does not automatically mean safe; discuss herbal products and supplements with your provider as they can have side effects and interactions.

Over-the-counter (OTC) products can be helpful for minor ailments, but older adults need to exercise extra caution due to potential risks, increased sensitivity to side effects, and interactions with other medications.

9.1 General Principles for OTC Use in Older Adults

  • Consult First: Always talk to your doctor or pharmacist before starting any new OTC drug, vitamin, or herbal supplement. They can help determine if it's safe and appropriate for you, considering your health conditions and current medications.
  • Read Labels: The "Drug Facts" label on OTC products contains crucial information. Read it carefully to understand active ingredients, uses, warnings, directions, and potential interactions.
  • Avoid Duplicate Ingredients: Check active ingredients to prevent accidental overdose. Many OTC products, especially multi-symptom cold and flu remedies, contain the same active ingredients (e.g., acetaminophen, ibuprofen, diphenhydramine). Taking multiple products with the same ingredient can be dangerous.
  • Treat Only Your Symptoms: Avoid combination products (often labeled "Multi-Symptom Relief" or "Complete Relief") if you do not have all the symptoms they are designed to treat. Choose single-ingredient products whenever possible to target specific symptoms.
  • Keep a Comprehensive Medication List: Maintain a full, updated list of everything you take—prescription drugs, OTC medications, vitamins, and herbal supplements—and share it with your healthcare providers at every visit.
  • Herbal and "Natural" Product Caution: "Natural" does not inherently mean "safe." Herbal products and dietary supplements are not regulated by the FDA in the same way as prescription or OTC drugs. They can cause side effects and interact with other medications. Always discuss their use with your doctor or pharmacist.
  • Watch for Overlaps and Problematic Ingredients:
    • Be aware that Pepto-Bismol contains a salicylate, similar to aspirin. Taking both concurrently increases the risk of bleeding.
    • Many cold and allergy medications, as well as some sleep aids, contain first-generation antihistamines (like diphenhydramine or chlorpheniramine), which should generally be avoided or used with extreme caution in older adults due to risks of confusion, drowsiness, dry mouth, constipation, and falls.

9.3 When to Seek Medical Care: Self-Care vs. Urgent Care vs. Emergency Room

It’s crucial to recognize when an illness or drug reaction can be managed at home with OTC treatments and rest, versus when professional medical evaluation is necessary. Any symptom that is severe, worsening, new and unexplained, or concerning for a serious condition should prompt a doctor’s visit. Older adults may present with atypical symptoms (e.g., a heart attack manifesting as fatigue or indigestion rather than classic chest pain), so erring on the side of caution is wise.

You can usually manage at home (self-care with OTC meds) if:

  • Symptoms are mild and there are no red flags (e.g., common cold with runny nose, mild cough, low-grade fever; minor headache relieved by acetaminophen; occasional heartburn managed by antacids; small scrape or bruise; mild, short-lived diarrhea). Use OTCs as directed, rest, and ensure adequate fluid intake. If symptoms persist longer than expected (e.g., cold >7-10 days, recurrent headache, frequent heartburn), call your doctor.
  • You experience mild, non-dangerous medication side effects (e.g., slight drowsiness from an OTC antihistamine taken at night). If a new medication (OTC or prescription) causes a skin rash, significant dizziness, or anything worrisome, stop the medicine and contact a healthcare provider.

Go to an Urgent Care clinic or call your doctor soon if:

  • You have a moderate illness or minor injury needing attention but not immediately life-threatening.
    • Examples: Suspected ear infection (ear pain, fever). Suspected urinary tract infection (burning urination, frequent urge). Bronchitis or persistent cough. Minor asthma flare-up (mild wheezing not in severe distress). Spreading or very uncomfortable rashes. Sprains or possible minor fractures (urgent care often has X-ray capabilities).
    • Fever over 101°F (38.3°C) lasting more than 2-3 days or accompanied by worsening symptoms like cough.
  • You experience a medication reaction that is concerning but not immediately life-threatening (e.g., a widespread rash after starting an antibiotic, but breathing is fine). If a new OTC med causes significant discomfort (e.g., severe stomach pain from a new heartburn medicine), stop it and seek advice.
  • Symptoms are in-between, or you're unsure and can't reach your regular doctor. Urgent care is a good option and can escalate to the ER if needed. This includes issues like dehydration from a stomach bug where you can't keep fluids down (urgent care may provide IV fluids).

Call 911 or Go to the Emergency Room (ER) immediately if:

  • You have severe, sudden, or potentially life-threatening symptoms. For seniors, these include:
    • Chest pain or pressure (potential heart attack), especially if lasting more than a few minutes or accompanied by sweating, nausea, shortness of breath. Even if unsure, it's safer to get it checked.
    • Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing (serious lung or heart issue). If asthma/COPD inhalers aren't helping, go to ER.
    • Stroke symptoms (F.A.S.T.): Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty (slurred, confused), Time to call 911. Also sudden severe headache, sudden vision loss. Time is critical.
    • Seizure or loss of consciousness.
    • Severe abdominal pain, especially if abrupt/intense, or with persistent vomiting, or a rigid/tender belly (could be appendicitis, pancreatitis, bowel blockage).
    • Uncontrolled bleeding (deep cut, coughing/vomiting blood).
    • Significant trauma (fall from height, bone break through skin, head injury with confusion/fainting).
    • Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): Swelling of face/lips/throat, difficulty breathing, hives, lightheadedness after a new medication, food, or sting. Call 911 immediately. Use an epinephrine auto-injector if available.
    • High fever (e.g., >103°F or 39.4°C) with serious symptoms like neck stiffness, confusion, severe headache, chest pain, difficulty breathing.
    • Signs of severe dehydration (very dry mouth, little/no urination, dizziness) and inability to keep fluids down.
    • Any symptom that worries you greatly – trust your instincts. For older adults, the threshold for ER evaluation is lower because conditions can escalate rapidly. A sudden onset of confusion, extreme weakness, or a general feeling that "something isn't right" can signify a serious underlying issue (like an infection) even without prominent fever or pain.
  • If you start to feel extremely unwell after taking any medication (OTC or prescription), including mental confusion, difficulty walking, or any severe, out-of-the-ordinary symptom. Medication effects can sometimes be more pronounced in older adults.

Don't downplay symptoms because you don't want to be a bother. It’s always okay to call your physician’s office and ask for guidance on whether to go to urgent care or the ER. Keep a phone nearby in case you need to call for help.

Chapter 11: Antibiotic Use: Finish the Course and Use Wisely

Did you know?

Antibiotics only work against bacterial infections, not viruses like the common cold or flu! Using them unnecessarily contributes to antibiotic resistance, making future infections harder to treat.

If your doctor prescribes an antibiotic to treat a bacterial infection, it is critical to use it correctly. Antibiotics can save lives, but their misuse can lead to significant problems, including antibiotic resistance.

Key Principles for Antibiotic Use:

  • Always finish the full prescribed course: Even if you feel better after a few days, do not stop taking the antibiotic early unless your healthcare provider explicitly tells you to. Stopping prematurely can leave behind bacteria that are not fully eradicated. These surviving bacteria may cause the infection to recur and could develop resistance to the antibiotic. Completing the entire course helps ensure that even the more resilient bacteria are killed.
  • Do not skip doses: Try to take antibiotic doses at evenly spaced intervals as instructed (e.g., "every 8 hours"). Missing doses or taking them inconsistently can also allow bacteria to survive and become resistant. If you accidentally miss a dose, ask your pharmacist or doctor for guidance.
  • Understand the consequences of misuse or overuse: Improper antibiotic use contributes to the development of antibiotic-resistant bacteria. These "superbugs" make infections much harder to treat, potentially requiring stronger intravenous medications, longer hospital stays, or, in worst-case scenarios, leading to untreatable infections. Antibiotic resistance is a serious global health threat. Overuse (e.g., taking antibiotics when not needed) also destroys beneficial bacteria in your body, which can lead to complications like C. difficile infection, a severe and difficult-to-treat diarrhea.
  • Use antibiotics only for bacterial infections: Antibiotics are ineffective against viruses, such as those that cause the common cold or flu. Taking antibiotics for viral illnesses will not help you recover and contributes to antibiotic resistance and unnecessary side effects. For instance, most cases of bronchitis are viral and do not require antibiotics. Trust your doctor's assessment; if they determine an antibiotic is not needed, do not pressure them for one.
  • Never use leftover antibiotics or someone else’s prescription: Do not save antibiotics from a previous illness to use later, and never take antibiotics prescribed for another person. Using the wrong medication, an incorrect dose, or an incomplete course will not effectively treat an infection and can be dangerous. Dispose of any leftover antibiotics properly (e.g., through a drug take-back program).
  • Be aware of side effects and interactions: Antibiotics can cause side effects such as diarrhea, nausea, or yeast infections, and they can interact with other medications. Take them exactly as directed (e.g., with food or on an empty stomach) to minimize issues. If you experience a concerning reaction (e.g., rash, difficulty breathing, severe diarrhea), seek medical attention. If a side effect is intolerable, call your doctor; they may be able to switch you to a different antibiotic rather than having you stop treatment altogether.

Preventing Antibiotic Resistance:

Everyone has a role in preventing antibiotic resistance:

  • Use antibiotics only when prescribed by a healthcare professional and follow their instructions precisely.
  • Do not demand antibiotics if your doctor says they are not necessary; inquire about alternative ways to manage your symptoms.
  • Keep up with recommended vaccinations (e.g., flu shot, pneumonia vaccine), as they can prevent illnesses that might otherwise lead to secondary bacterial infections requiring antibiotics.
  • Practice good hygiene, such as regular hand-washing and proper wound care, to help prevent infections in the first place.

By using antibiotics responsibly, you protect your own health and help ensure these vital medications remain effective for future generations.

Managing medications effectively in older adulthood is a cornerstone of maintaining health and quality of life. By working together, we can achieve safer and more effective medication use, leading to better health outcomes and an improved quality of life for older adults. Being informed is empowering—it helps individuals use medications safely, avoid common pitfalls, and seek the right help when needed.

Glossary

  • Adherence: Taking medicines exactly as prescribed.
  • Adverse Drug Event (ADE): Harm from medicine use.
  • Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API): Therapeutic component.
  • Anticholinergic: Drugs blocking acetylcholine; can cause dry mouth, confusion, etc.
  • Anticholinergic Burden: Cumulative effect of anticholinergic drugs.
  • Beers Criteria®: List of potentially inappropriate meds for older adults.
  • Bioequivalence: Generic delivers same amount of drug as brand.
  • Brand-name Drug: Original drug under trademarked name.
  • Compounding Pharmacy: Prepares customized medications.
  • Controlled Substance: Regulated drug due to abuse potential.
  • DEA Schedules: Classification of controlled substances.
  • Deprescribing: Planned reduction/stopping of meds.
  • Drug-Drug Interaction: One drug affects another.
  • Dysphagia: Difficulty swallowing.
  • Enteric-Coated (EC): Coating to prevent stomach dissolution.
  • Extended-Release (ER, XR, etc.): Slow medication release.
  • FDA: U.S. Food and Drug Administration.
  • Feeding Tube (Enteral Feeding Tube): Medical device for nutrition/meds.
  • Generic Drug: Same as brand-name in form, safety, strength, etc.
  • Hypoglycemia: Low blood sugar.
  • Multimorbidity: Two or more chronic health conditions.
  • OTC (Over-the-Counter): Meds without prescription.
  • Pill Burden: Total pills and management effort.
  • Polypharmacy: Use of multiple (often ≥5) medications.
  • Sublingual (SL): Medication dissolved under tongue.
  • Withdrawal Symptoms: Unpleasant feelings when stopping a medicine.
